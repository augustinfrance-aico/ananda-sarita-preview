Side note — for Augus

What delivery actually means

Short glossary so the word stops being vague. When Nikola says "delivery is the hard part", here's what's really behind the sentence.

1. It's not "launching the ads"

Anyone can press a blue button on Meta Business Manager. Delivery = making that ad actually return money. That requires knowing: audience layering, creative testing, bid strategies, pixel events, conversion tracking, budget allocation by funnel stage, reading cost-per-result → LTV → payback period.

2. A real Facebook media buyer spends their day doing this

  • Morning (1 h) — reviews every active campaign's results overnight. Kills losers, scales winners 10–20 %.
  • Midday (2 h) — briefs 3–5 new creatives per week to video editor / designer. Writes hooks. Scripts UGC angles.
  • Afternoon (2 h) — rebuilds audiences, tests new interests, launches new ad sets. Reads Meta's latest update docs.
  • Late (1 h) — writes client report. Explains what moved, what didn't, what's next.

Multiply that × 5 accounts = a full-time job for a senior, or 3 bad part-timers. Augus doing funnels ≠ Augus doing this. Two different crafts.

3. Why "delivery" is the bottleneck

Sales = Nikola's gift. Closing is fast. Delivery = the 30-day marathon after the close, when the client watches the dashboard every morning and thinks "is this working?". If the answer isn't clearly yes within 6 weeks, they churn. Delivery is what retains revenue.

Selling is sprinting to the starting line. Delivery is the race itself. — The distinction

Chapter Nine

Guardrails & decisions

The seven things that can kill this plan — and the rules to avoid each of them.

  • Don't hire before $8k MRR. Salaries are lethal until the engine is predictable. Freelancers until then.
  • No founder > 15 hours / week on a single client. If it happens, hire or fire.
